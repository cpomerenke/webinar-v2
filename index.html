Context
The Landscape Has Changed — Dramatically
2025 brought three simultaneous shifts that changed the stakes for every NDIS provider in Australia.
⚖️
Wage Theft Criminalised
From 1 January 2025, intentional underpayment became a criminal offence under the Fair Work Act. Penalties reach $7.8M for companies, or 10 years imprisonment for individuals. Even civil penalties now routinely exceed the original shortfall — in one landmark case, a $650K underpayment attracted $13.7M in penalties.
🚨 Effective 1 January 2025
🔍
FWO Inquiry Launched
The Fair Work Ombudsman launched a formal multi-year inquiry into the disability support sector in July 2025 — naming it a priority area with "widespread non-compliance." Uniting Communities was hit with an enforceable undertaking to repay $2.6M to 1,500 employees after a SCHADS Award audit spanning a decade.
📌 Multi-year priority enforcement
Hall & Wilcox — FWO Case Update NDS — Sector Inquiry
📄
SCHADS More Complex
Pay rates increased 3.5% from July 2025, then a second wave for aged care workers hit in October 2025. New classification structures, ERO loadings, home care stream split, and an ongoing gender undervaluation review mean this award is changing faster than ever.
🔄 Rate changes twice in 2025

Pillar 4 · Compliance Risk
What Happens When Something Goes Wrong
Manual SCHADS payroll isn't just inefficient — it's a risk ladder. Every rung is a real consequence FWO providers have faced.
🔍
Stage 1 — Employee complaint or self-audit reveals error
A broken shift, travel allowance, or penalty rate has been miscalculated. Could be one employee — or 200.
📋
Stage 2 — FWO issues a Compliance Notice
Even a $7,800 underpayment (like Agape, 2024) triggers formal notice. You must calculate and repay — under FWO supervision.
⚖️
Stage 3 — Civil penalty proceedings
For unintentional underpayments. Maximum penalty: up to $4,950,000 or 3× the underpayment — whichever is greater.
🚨
Stage 4 — Criminal prosecution (from Jan 2025)
Intentional or reckless underpayment = criminal offence. Up to 10 years imprisonment for individuals. Directors personally liable under s550.
